# Sales Patterns — Optimus Business Solutions
# Distilled from real closed deals. Update after every sales meeting.

## Demo Structure That Closes (Validated: Placed Right Fence, Apr 2026)

Order matters. This sequence closed a premium package (complete build) in one in-person meeting.

### 1. Open with Research
Lead with strategic recommendations pulled from the market research report.
Show them you already understand their market better than they do.
This establishes expertise before the product is revealed.

> "Here are some strategic recommendations from the report: replace stock photos with
> real photography, publish transparent pricing, implement response-time communication..."

This positions you as a consultant, not a vendor.

---

### 2. Demo the Hero First
The animated hero is the hook. Show it before anything else.
Let the client react. Don't talk over it. Give it a beat.

> "There we are — the last fence you'll ever need."

Custom canvas/JS animations on the hero are the single biggest "wow" moment.
They get shown to other business owners. They close deals.

---

### 3. Walk the One-Click Checkout Flow
Show the primary CTA at the top. Then demo the estimate form.
The message: ease of use = money.

> "The whole point of the difference between a high converting website and an average
> website is the ease of use... one click checkout."

Demo it as a buyer would experience it: pick fence type → optional linear footage →
timeline → submit. Emphasize that it goes directly to their inbox.

---

### 4. Show the Gallery with Filtering
If you built type-based filtering (wood / vinyl / aluminum), demo it.
One-click filter. Let them feel the speed.

> "Say they want to just check out the wood ones. One click, now only wood ones."

---

### 5. Show the Quiz
Position it as: "so they don't have to read the whole website."
This is especially powerful for clients who worry about confusing buyers.

---

### 6. Show the Shop (if applicable)
Demo accessories and merch. The key move: suggest product categories they hadn't
thought of ("fence lights — install them at the time of installation").

> "Adding those accessories in the shop would be extremely powerful."

Let the client's own product ideas surface. Validate them. It makes the shop feel
personal and theirs, not generic.

---

### 7. ROI Calculator — Always Last Before Pricing
Never show pricing first. Show the ROI calculator immediately before pricing.
Frame the investment as a simulation, not a cost.

Structure:
- Leads per month the site is projected to generate (use conservative number)
- Conversion rate (use 30–50% — intentionally low to undersell and overdeliver)
- Average project value (pull from their data)
- Resulting monthly revenue → annual revenue → first-year ROI %

> "The analytics say on average you'll get about eight extra leads a month...
> say you only convert 30%... $5,200 average project...
> predicted $12,480 extra monthly revenue, $130,000 extra annual revenue,
> 2,623% first-year return on your investment."

**Key framing:** "And we're using low averages for that." Let them mentally inflate it.

---

### 8. Three-Tier Pricing Table
Present all three tiers. Name them clearly: Starter → Growth → Complete.
Growth is the "most popular" — badge it.
Complete is the anchor. Don't badge it. Its job is to make Growth feel like a deal.

Walk each tier briefly. Don't oversell — the ROI calculator already did the work.

---

### 9. Tax Write-Off + Credit Card Close
After pricing, immediately offer this:

> "I used to be a finance guy. Highly recommend you put it on a credit card.
> Take that receipt to your accountant — it's a business expense, necessary
> and ordinary to your business. Marketing, advertising. If you get the [price]
> package, instead of paying [X] in taxes, you pay [X minus package cost].
> It's the best way to do it."

This converts the price from a cost to a tax event. Works for any business owner.

---

### 10. Send the Invoice During the Meeting
Do not wait. Do not follow up later. Send it while you're sitting with them.

> "I just sent it to you now."

When they see it arrive in real time, the close is already done.

## Upsell Sequence — Three Phases to Sell

Website is Phase 1. Introduce Phase 2 and 3 at the right moment — not before the
foundation is sold, and not buried in the pitch. Mention them briefly when the client
surfaces the need organically (e.g., they ask about automation or ads).

| Phase | Product | When to Introduce |
|-------|---------|------------------|
| 1 | Website (foundation) | Today's meeting |
| 2 | Marketing (ads, social content) | "Once we're launched, we'll start talking" |
| 3 | Automation (CRM, instant follow-up workflows) | When client mentions missing leads or slow response |

> "This is our foundation. There's still so much we can build."

Never pitch all three at once. The website must exist before automation matters.

## Demo Site Access as Pre-Payment Hook

Client: "Is there a way to look at the demo site from home so I can show Roger?"
Response: "Yeah, absolutely. And once the invoice is paid, it's yours."

The demo site creates desire. Payment unlocks access. Use this deliberately.

## In-Meeting Infrastructure Setup (Validated: Sylvia Rich, Apr 2026)

Don't just close the sale — also do the onboarding during the meeting.
After the invoice is paid, immediately set up:

1. **GoDaddy domain purchase** — search the name together, let the client feel ownership
2. **Resend account** — create it live, get the API key, set up email delivery
3. **GoDaddy admin access** — grant yourself access right there so you can configure DNS at home

> "Will save us a whole week."

This removes the #1 post-sale friction point (client never gets around to it).
It also locks the client in psychologically — they just bought infrastructure, not just a website.

**Domain search tip:** Do it together. Let them discover the name with you.
Start with their preferred name. If it's taken, redirect to a shorter variant.
Explain the difference between 1-year and 3-year pricing live.

## Let the Client Play the Quiz During Demo

Don't demo the quiz as a narrator. Hand it to the client.

> "I'm gonna pretend I'm one of your people."

Walk them through it as their own customer. They experience the journey their clients will have.
This is the most effective way to sell the quiz feature — they feel it from both sides.

## Gap Analysis as the Demo Opener (Validated: Sylvia Rich, Apr 2026)

Before showing the site, present the research gap analysis.
Not your site's features. Their category's failures.

Structure:
- "Gap #1 that we can fill: [specific thing no competitor has done]"
- "Gap #2..."
- etc.

Name specific competitor sites and what they're missing. Be specific — not "bad design" but
"no one across all honorary consul websites explains the distinction between honorary consul
services and full consulate services."

This establishes you as a category expert before the product reveal.
The site then becomes the solution to a set of confirmed problems, not just a pretty thing.

## AEO as Urgency Trigger (Validated: Sylvia Rich, Apr 2026)

After explaining SEO, introduce AEO (AI Engine Optimization):

> "There is SEO — when you type it into Google. And there is AEO — AI Engine Optimization —
> which is pretty much search engine optimization but for chat. So when ChatGPT goes to
> answer a query, it's instantly looking at the whole internet and finding what you need.
> This site will make you found. And we can also focus in on your AEO and optimize that."

**When to use it:** Ask if they've ever searched themselves or their niche in ChatGPT.
If the wrong person comes up (old, retired, or competitor), this becomes an immediate urgency trigger.
Getting live fast is no longer just a preference — it's a strategic race.

## Save the Phone Demo for Last (Validated: Sylvia Rich, Apr 2026)

After walking through the full desktop site, close with:

> "And I didn't even show you the best part."

Then pull out the phone and show the mobile view.
If the site is genuinely mobile-optimized, this lands hard — especially against competitors
whose mobile versions are broken or unusable.

> "It's almost mandatory in 2026."

Position it as an industry standard they'd be ahead of, not a feature they're paying extra for.

---

## Blog = Time Savings Framing (Validated: Sylvia Rich, Apr 2026)

Don't pitch the blog as content marketing. Pitch it as an email filter.

Identify the client's single most common time-wasting inquiry.
Show a blog article that directly answers it.

> "Your website should answer every possible question that they need to book — and not bother you."

Then: "If I get that email again, I just send them here."

This makes the blog feel like a tool that reduces work, not creates it.
Especially powerful for service businesses who are drowning in the same basic questions.

## Pain Points That Accelerate Close

Listen for these in discovery. If they surface organically, engage immediately:

| Pain | What it signals | How to respond |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| "My form doesn't send notifications" | Active lead loss | Show the estimate form → "leads go right to your inbox" |
| "I'm not a coder" | Fear of abandonment | Emphasize support: "I'm gonna be there for you the whole way" |
| "I'm on Yelp but it's not working" | Wasted ad spend | Segue to traffic ownership and website-embedded tools |
| "I had to check the form manually to find the lead" | Systems problem | Intro automation upsell |
