## ROI / Value Framing

**Investment, not a cost:**
> "So now, let's see how much money you're gonna make."

**Conservative framing (let them inflate mentally):**
> "And we're using low averages for that. We are using little average... you want to
> aim low for certainty."

**Credit card + tax write-off close:**
> "I used to be a finance guy. Highly recommend you put it on a credit card.
> Take that receipt to your accountant. Tell them it's a business expense —
> necessary and ordinary to your business. Marketing, advertising.
> If you get the [X] package, instead of paying [Y] in taxes, you'll pay [Y minus X].
> It's the best way to do it. I recommend that to all my clients."

## Responding to "What Does It Cost?"

Don't answer with a number first. Answer with ROI, then tiers.

1. ROI calculator: show projected monthly/annual revenue + first-year return %
2. Then: "So now you're probably asking — what is it gonna cost me?"
3. Present all three tiers
4. Let the middle tier (Growth) do the work
5. Close with credit card + tax write-off advice

## AEO (AI Engine Optimization) Explanation

Use when client already uses or understands ChatGPT. Ask if they've ever searched themselves in ChatGPT.

> "There is search engine optimization — so when you type it into Google. And there is
> AEO — AI Engine Optimization — which is the newest, pretty much search engine
> optimization but for chat. So when ChatGPT goes to answer that, it's instantly looking
> at the whole internet and finding what you need. This will make you found.
> And we can also focus in on your AEO and optimize that as well."

**Use case:** Client had searched "honorary consul of Hungary" in ChatGPT and the old (retired) consul came up. This immediately made "getting live fast" feel urgent — it's a race.

## Gap Analysis Opener

Use before showing the site. Positions as research-backed expert, not a vendor.

> "There's one main part I want to show you — feature and content gap analysis.
> Gap number one that we can fill: [specific page/content]. Not one [competitor type]
> has [this]. The [Competitor A] site does [X with no context]. The [Competitor B] site
> buries this in dense text. The [Competitor C] site assumes visitors already understand
> the hierarchy. So we're going to fill that gap."

Frame each gap as a confirmed market failure, not a feature pitch.

## In-Meeting Onboarding Language

**Framing domain purchase:**
> "So you already have the house. It's like backwards from real life — on the internet,
> you first build the house and then you put it on the address. So now that we have the
> house in the digital space, we need to give it an address to live at. That's what
> the domain's for."

**After setup complete:**
> "So those are the only things we needed to do. This will save us a whole week."

**On granting admin access:**
> "I need to grant myself administrative access so I can configure your DNS settings.
> It's quite an extensive process, but I can do that at home."

## Bilingual Feature Positioning

**For any client with a non-English-speaking customer base:**
> "One click — the entire site is instantly in [language]. All of it.
> And it's formal [language] — trained specifically for your audience."

**Cultural specificity matters:** For Hungarian, flag that it must be third-person formal
(like old-world formal European address — "one would like to"). This is a significant
cultural differentiator that no generic AI translation handles correctly.
