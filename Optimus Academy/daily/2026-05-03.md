### Detailed Notes

#### Series framing — "lazy leverage" + the last-mile thesis

sourcetms opens by naming the series: **lazy leverage** — minimum work, maximum quality output, via AI. The opening thesis: **humans are the last mile of large language models.** The analogy is logistics — the 90% of any supply chain is built by massive infrastructure (warehouses, freight, distribution); the last 10% (last-mile delivery) is where the actual job happens. Same shape with LLMs: the model does the 90%, the human does the 10% that takes generic output and turns it into something worth shipping. The five skills below are all framed against this thesis.

(Note: the speaker's article version is offered via DM if you comment "article" on the post — not retrieved here, but flagged as potential follow-up enrichment if the topic recurs.)

#### Skill 1 — Managing morphing systems

The skill: create explicit STRUCTURE around your work AND treat it as **modular** — pieces switching in and out. The components could be:
- AI models (swap in/out as new releases land)
- People
- Skill files (Claude/agent skills)
- Environments (different IDEs, different runtimes, different orchestrators)

**The non-negotiable:** don't get attached to your work. As new tools/models/skills evolve, throw out what you have and replace it. The work product is downstream; the system is upstream. Manage the system, replace the components.

Applies to every role — marketer, developer, executive, artisan. Not a developer-only skill.

#### Skill 2 — Orchestrating agents

The skill: direct multiple agents toward a shared goal. The speaker uses **conductor.build** — described as an "agent orchestration platform primarily used for coding, but because coding agents have become so good, we can use them now in operational work." Implication: as coding agents mature, more operational work becomes programmatic — whether you like it or not.

**Tactical:** give agents a shared goal. Use an orchestrator OR sub-agents OR a single agent — depends on advancement level. Mix-and-match the underlying tools (Codex, Claude Code, Devin, Kimi, Cowork, etc.) — these are the *method and environment* of orchestration, not the orchestration itself. The skill is in directing them well to achieve the goal, not in any particular tool.

#### Skill 3 — Think like an architect

The skill: the last-mile human's job is to **research, plan, and review** — not to be the executor. You want to be "the most efficient bundle of knowledge, direction, execution for a language model." Do all the context-gathering up front. Either dump it on the agent in advance OR let the agent interview you to extract it. Then hand it off.

**Critical contrast:** firing off lots of tasks generates lots of stuff (text, assets, output) but quality erodes. Architect-mode planning is the throttle — methodical, evaluating the whole structure and individual pieces, sensing the "vibe" of what's being built. An architect plans the building; an architect doesn't pour concrete.

**Tactical:** have an agent extract knowledge from you (via interview) BEFORE the task starts. Plan first; execute second.

#### Skill 4 — Taste and self-extraction

The opening analogy: wealthy/successful people often have good taste because their foundations are covered (food, income, basics) and they have cognitive surplus to evaluate their experiences ("how was that service? did I enjoy this product?"). When base needs aren't disrupting your processing, you can perceive quality.

Apply that pattern professionally: notice **how people are experiencing your work** (services, products, outputs). What makes them excited? What do they like? Then **use AI (visual or text models) to extract that information out of you.** Your job — the last-mile human job — is to externalize your taste so the LLM can amplify it.

**Tactical move:** assemble a **corpus of yourself** — favorite writing, vision boards, scrapbooks, photographs, design choices. Upload to an AI. Ask it to identify, analyze, and extract "what makes you you" — that becomes branding, personalization, the asset layer. Speaker's own example: he loves ASCII art and uses it as section markers in his videos and across the Supercenter website. ASCII art is the "thing beholden to him" — the more he can identify those things, the more he can use them as assets in a system.

**The compounding loop:** human creativity + artificial creativity → exponentially more output. New output creates new assets. New assets feed a new system. Continue.

#### Skill 5 — Compound engineering

Credit: the speaker learned this from **Dan Shipper at every.to**.

Definition: **writing a little bit of code or a single prompt and having multiple events cascade like a waterfall off it.** Token-expensive in the short term but extremely efficient — once an agent has guardrails inside a system you've devised, downstream actions run with confidence.

**Coding example:** the speaker has ONE project that contains his website, his application, his branding, his email messaging, his personality. An AI model can search the entire documentation, grab everything, follow the comments-for-agents he's left to navigate. The whole substrate is one searchable corpus.

**Marketing example (analogous):** integrate different tools using connectors. Same shape — once the system is wired, one prompt cascades through the whole pipeline.

**The general definition:** "compound engineering is essentially building your profession into an AI engineer for every task that you do." The substrates: devised prompts, skill files, taste documents, style references, artifacts — *everything* you have, made AI-accessible.

#### Closing thesis — every profession is becoming AI engineering

The summary: these skills are "actually the skills built and required by AI engineers. But more and more roles and professions are becoming AI engineers." The implication for non-engineers: the meta-skills above transfer regardless of original profession. Marketers who learn this become AI marketers. Executives who learn this become AI executives. Artisans who learn this become AI artisans.
