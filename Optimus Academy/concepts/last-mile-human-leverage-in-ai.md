# Last-Mile Human Leverage in AI

> **Concept distilled from:**
> - [[../daily/2026-05-03#10:26 — "I made this video to educate you on the five essential skills in 2026..." by sourcetms]] — sourcetms
>
> **Last updated:** 2026-05-03 10:26

## What it is

A meta-philosophy of working with large language models: the LLM does the 90%, the human does the last 10% that turns generic output into quality work. The five compounding skills that make a human effective in that role — **managing morphing systems, orchestrating agents, thinking like an architect, taste / self-extraction, and compound engineering** — are framed as the essential AI-era career skills, applicable across any profession (marketer, developer, executive, artisan).

## When to use

- When designing a personal or organizational workflow that depends on AI tools and agents — the five skills are operating principles, not optional polish.
- When deciding where to spend your own time relative to an AI agent's time — the architect-mode skill answers: more on planning and review, less on execution.
- When evaluating whether a profession (any profession) is being absorbed by AI engineering — the closing thesis (every role is becoming an AI-engineering role) is a forecast about how to allocate skill-building energy over the next 1-3 years.
- When auditing existing systems for fragility — the morphing-systems skill says: are your components swappable, or are they wedged in a way that locks you into yesterday's tools?
- When trying to articulate brand voice, taste, or "what makes our work feel like our work" — the taste / self-extraction skill is the operating recipe (build a corpus, ask an AI to extract patterns, render those as reusable assets).
- When you keep generating volume from AI but quality is uneven — the architect-mode + compound-engineering skills together are the discipline upgrade.

## Mechanics

The unifying thesis is the **last-mile delivery analogy**. In logistics, the 90% of any supply chain is built by massive infrastructure (warehouses, freight, distribution); the last 10% (last-mile delivery) is where the actual job happens. Same shape with LLMs: the model does the 90%, the human does the 10% that takes generic output and turns it into something worth shipping. Every skill below is a sub-pattern of being effective at that last 10%.

### Sub-skill 1 — Managing morphing systems

Create explicit STRUCTURE around your work AND treat it as **modular** — pieces switching in and out.

The components in a morphing system can be:
- AI models (swap in/out as new releases land)
- People (team composition shifts)
- Skill files (Claude/agent skills evolve)
- Environments (different IDEs, runtimes, orchestrators)

The non-negotiable corollary: **don't get attached to your work.** As new tools/models/skills evolve, throw out what you have and replace it. The work product is downstream; the system is upstream. Manage the system, replace the components.

This skill is universal — marketers, developers, executives, artisans all benefit. It's not a developer-specific skill, despite tooling examples often coming from developer contexts.

### Sub-skill 2 — Orchestrating agents

Direct multiple agents toward a shared goal. The substrate options:
- Single agent (the simple case)
- Sub-agents under a single orchestrator
- Full orchestration platforms (e.g., conductor.build, OpenClaw, Cowork, Claude Code, Codex, Devin, Kimi)

The skill is in directing the agents well to achieve the goal — not in any particular tool. Tools are method-and-environment; orchestration discipline is the actual skill.

A specific 2026 trend the source flags: as coding agents mature, more operational work becomes programmatic. Orchestration originally a coding-agent pattern, now applies to ops, marketing, and any workflow with structured steps.

### Sub-skill 3 — Think like an architect

The last-mile human's job is to **research, plan, and review** — not to be the executor. Be "the most efficient bundle of knowledge, direction, execution for a language model."

The discipline:
1. Do all the context-gathering up front.
2. Either dump the context on the agent OR let the agent **interview you to extract it.**
3. Hand off the plan; let the agent execute.

The contrast that motivates this skill: firing off lots of tasks generates lots of stuff (text, assets, output) but quality erodes when the planning step is skipped. Architect-mode planning is the throttle — methodical, evaluating the whole structure and individual pieces, sensing the "vibe" of what's being built. *An architect plans the building; an architect doesn't pour concrete.*

The "agent interviews you before the task" pattern is the most actionable shape — flip the default from "I dump context once" to "the agent extracts what it needs from me" so the planning phase becomes interactive and the gaps surface before execution.

### Sub-skill 4 — Taste and self-extraction

The opening analogy: wealthy/successful people often have good taste because their foundations are covered (food, income, basics) and they have cognitive surplus to evaluate their experiences ("how was that service? did I enjoy this product?"). When base needs aren't disrupting your processing, you can perceive quality.

Apply the same shape professionally: notice **how people are experiencing your work** (services, products, outputs). What makes them excited? What do they like? Then **use AI (visual or text models) to extract that information out of you.** The last-mile human job here is to externalize taste so the LLM can amplify it.

The mechanic — assemble a **corpus of yourself**:
- Favorite writing
- Vision boards and scrapbooks
- Photographs documenting your aesthetic
- Design choices
- Past work you're proud of

Upload to an AI. Ask it to identify, analyze, and extract "what makes you you." Output becomes branding, personalization, the asset layer — usable across every future task.

The compounding loop:
```
human creativity + artificial creativity
  → exponentially more output
  → new output becomes new assets
  → new assets feed a new system
  → continue
```

The source's own example: the speaker loves ASCII art and uses it as section markers in his videos and across the Supercenter website. ASCII art is the "thing beholden to him" — the more he can identify those things, the more he can use them as reusable assets in a system.

### Sub-skill 5 — Compound engineering

Credited to **Dan Shipper at every.to** as the source of the term.

Definition: **writing a little bit of code or a single prompt and having multiple events cascade like a waterfall off it.** Token-expensive in the short term but extremely efficient — once an agent has guardrails inside a system you've devised, downstream actions run with confidence.

The general principle: "building your profession into an AI engineer for every task you do." Substrates that compound:
- Devised prompts (parameterized, reusable)
- Skill files
- Taste documents and style references
- Artifacts (templates, examples, comments-for-agents left in code)
- Single-substrate projects where the entire context (website, application, branding, email messaging, personality) is co-located so an AI model can search and grab everything in one pass

The structural example the source gives — one project containing website + application + branding + email messaging + personality, with inline "comments for agents" left throughout to help an AI model navigate. The whole substrate is one searchable corpus, and a single prompt can cascade through all of it.

The same pattern translates to non-coding domains: a marketing system integrating different tools via connectors, where one prompt cascades through enrichment → personalization → outbound → CRM logging, is compound engineering applied to revenue ops.

### Closing thesis — every profession is becoming AI engineering

These five skills are framed as the skills required of AI engineers — but the source's claim is broader: "more and more roles and professions are becoming AI engineers." The implication: the meta-skills above transfer regardless of original profession. Marketers who learn this become AI marketers. Executives who learn this become AI executives. Artisans who learn this become AI artisans.

The forecast horizon the source uses: end of 2026 as the deadline to feel "confident and comfortable using AI" via these skills. By implication, late adopters past that horizon are operating at a meaningful disadvantage in any profession.

## Examples

**The single-project compound-engineering substrate** (what sourcetms maintains for himself):
```
project/
  ├── website/
  ├── application/
  ├── branding/
  ├── email-messaging/
  └── personality/
```
Plus inline "comments for agents" left throughout to help an AI model navigate the substrate. One prompt → cascade through the whole thing.

**The taste / self-extraction prompt shape** (paraphrased from the speaker's framing):
```
Given this corpus (my writing, vision boards, photographs, design choices),
identify, analyze, and extract what makes me me. Surface the patterns I use
repeatedly that feel beholden to me. Render those as assets I can reuse.
```

**The architect's interview pattern** (Skill 3, applied):
```
Before executing the task, interview me to extract everything you need:
- What is the goal?
- What context do I have that you don't?
- What examples or prior work should you reference?
- What does "done" look like?
- What's the worst-case output I want to avoid?

Once you've extracted enough, restate the plan and confirm it before
producing anything.
```

## Gotchas

- **Volume without architect-mode planning produces noise, not quality.** The source is explicit: "if you fire off tons of tasks, you're creating a lot of stuff... but is any of that quality? Will that hold up over time?" The five skills only compound if planning precedes execution.
- **Attachment to current tools breaks Skill 1.** The morphing-systems discipline requires throwing away components as new ones evolve. If you're emotionally attached to a particular orchestrator, model, or skill file because you built it, you'll resist the swap that the system needs.
- **Compound engineering is token-expensive in the short term.** The cascade pattern uses more tokens per prompt than a single targeted call. The efficiency win is in elapsed time and quality, not in raw API cost. If your bottleneck is API spend rather than engineering throughput, the math may not justify it yet.
- **Taste extraction requires raw material.** Skill 4's mechanic depends on having a corpus to feed the AI. If you don't yet have favorite writing, design references, or past work documented, the skill is gated on building the corpus first. There's no shortcut.
- **The "every profession is becoming AI engineering" claim is partially marketing.** It's true directionally but exaggerated as a near-term universal — many traditional professions (in-person service, regulated trades, physical labor) absorb AI engineering more slowly than knowledge work does. Don't pivot a whole career strategy on the strongest possible read of this thesis without checking the timeline against your actual domain.
- **Skill 2 (orchestrating agents) without Skill 3 (think like an architect) produces fast garbage.** Orchestrating multiple agents amplifies whatever direction you give them. Bad direction × multiple agents = bad output × N. The architect-mode discipline is what makes orchestration produce quality rather than just volume.
- **The speaker's tool list (conductor.build, Codex, Claude Code, Devin, Kimi, Cowork) is illustrative, not endorsement-tested.** Treat the list as evidence that orchestration is real, not as a curated stack. Each tool has its own evaluation gate before adoption (per `feedback_mission-trumps-stack-loyalty.md`).
